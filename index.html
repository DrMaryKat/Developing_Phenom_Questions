Welcome to Week 2

Welcome back! Last week, we explored the philosophical foundations of phenomenology. This week, we take a crucial step toward becoming phenomenological researchers: learning to formulate research questions that are truly phenomenological in nature.

This might sound simple, but it's actually one of the most common places where phenomenological studies go wrong. Many dissertations claim to be phenomenological but ask questions that don't fit the methodology. By the end of this week, you'll understand exactly what makes a research question phenomenological—and you'll have drafted your own.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish phenomenological research questions from other types of qualitative research questions
  2. Explain how phenomenological research questions align with the philosophy of phenomenology
  3. Identify the essential components of a well-formulated phenomenological research question
  4. Evaluate research questions for their phenomenological appropriateness
  5. Draft preliminary phenomenological research questions for your own study

Why This Matters

Your research question is the foundation of your entire study. A poorly formulated question leads to methodological confusion, data that doesn't fit your analysis approach, and findings that don't contribute to phenomenological understanding. Getting this right from the start saves enormous frustration later.

Key Concepts This Week

Phenomenological Question
Asks about the lived experience of a phenomenon
Phenomenon
A specific, identifiable experience to be studied
Participants
People who have lived the phenomenon
Essence
The core meaning or structure of an experience

The Phenomenological Research Question Formula

Phenomenological research questions follow a specific structure that keeps the focus on lived experience:

The Basic Formula
"What is the lived experience of [phenomenon] for [participants]?"
Variations include: "What is it like to experience...?" or "What is the meaning of the experience of...?"

What Makes a Question Phenomenological?

A truly phenomenological research question has these characteristics:

Characteristic Explanation
Focuses on lived experience Asks what it is like to experience something, not just what happened
Identifies a specific phenomenon Names a particular experience (not a topic, population, or theory)
Seeks meaning and essence Aims to understand the core structure or meaning of the experience
Is open and exploratory Does not assume outcomes or test hypotheses
Requires first-person accounts Can only be answered by those who have lived the experience

Examples of Phenomenological Research Questions

Learning to recognize good phenomenological research questions is a skill. Study these examples carefully.

Well-Formulated Phenomenological Questions

Good Example

"What is the lived experience of first-generation college students navigating the transition to university life?"

Why it works: Identifies a clear phenomenon (navigating the transition), specifies participants who have lived it (first-generation students), and focuses on experience rather than outcomes or causes.

Good Example

"What is it like for principals to lead their schools through a sudden shift to remote learning?"

Why it works: Uses "what is it like" phrasing, identifies a specific phenomenon (leading through sudden shift), and focuses on the experiential dimension rather than strategies or outcomes.

Good Example

"What is the meaning of the experience of receiving a learning disability diagnosis as an adult?"

Why it works: Seeks meaning (a phenomenological goal), identifies a specific moment/phenomenon (receiving diagnosis), and specifies participants (adults, not children).

Questions That Need Revision

Needs Revision

"What strategies do first-generation students use to succeed in college?"

The problem: Asks about strategies (behaviors), not lived experience. This is a good qualitative question, but it's not phenomenological. Better: "What is the lived experience of academic success for first-generation college students?"

Needs Revision

"How does teacher burnout affect student achievement?"

The problem: Asks about cause and effect, not lived experience. This is better suited for quantitative or mixed methods research. Better: "What is the lived experience of burnout for elementary school teachers?"

Needs Revision

"What do teachers think about professional development?"

The problem: Asks for opinions/perceptions rather than lived experience. Better: "What is the lived experience of participating in transformative professional development?"

Common Pitfall

Many students confuse "perceptions" with "lived experience." Asking what people think about something is different from asking what it is like to experience something. Phenomenology focuses on the latter.

Draft Your Research Question
Research Development • 300-400 words
20 pts

Building on your phenomenon brainstorm from last week, draft a preliminary phenomenological research question for your study. Include:

  • Your draft research question (following the phenomenological formula)
  • The phenomenon you are studying (be specific)
